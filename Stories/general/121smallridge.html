“Shhh!”

My mother pursed her lips together and tip toed into my room, gesturing me to keep quiet.

“Oh...Kay”

She tip-toed out of the room again and came back with a broom. Gesturing me to keep quiet, she slunk towards my cupboard. And, ah! Now I saw it- There was a lizard in the room and it was in a precarious position- One false move and it would get into the cupboard where its family will grow exponentially and sending it out then would mean bringing down the whole cupboard. It was a fat and big one. The movements were deft, and with successful feigning my mother took the better hold of it. Catching it unawares, it did not have time to react. Frantically, it hurried out of the room, although only after playing its share of ploy- the reptile tried to get into every nook and corner, giving my mother a heartache every time.

Its movements were shoddy and clumsy and its wriggly movements made me irascible. Only when it was out of the house, I felt a slight remorse . The sight, where the lizard, scared to death crawled frantically with its life in plight but all that my mother did was to send it out as a challenge, or rather, a sport questioned my conscience. In a few moments, a vivid realization sunk in; sad, but true, it was only but a small ridge in my mind that kept me from reveling at the lizard’s struggle- or for the matter any repugnant thing. And the human mind can take anything at all to be repugnant without a reason. And all the hate-kills, they don't come as a surprise given that each of us has a monster within. The world would be a better place to live if each of us reigned over the animal nature in us.

Now about something that conceived in my mind's eye following the incident:

It was in the wee hours of morning. A fair-toned man tried to get some sleep as he tossed about in the bed. He lay there idle and irate till it was bright enough for him to be up. He got up, did his morning ablutions and went to the balustrade from where he could see miles ahead. As he looked out at his quarters in the cold, pristine morning, pride welled inside him. As he admired, a movement caught his eye. Down below, a dark toned woman shuffled her way across the street to dump garbage. The man grew crusty at the sight. His brows knitted together as he eyed her. As she drew closer he saw that she was covered with sweat, head to toe and her clothing stuck to her body. His contempt grew. Irate, he looked around. He saw his dog-trainer bathing his gargantuan hounds. Pausing, he gave a shrill whistle. The hounds looked up at him with hungry eyes. In a swift fashion, he gestured the hounds to charge her. The hounds, howling in ravage cacophony started the chase in glee. It did not take but a moment for the woman to realize what had happened. Scared to death, her eyes grew wide with panic. She had just turned around to save herself when the hounds charged her down to the ground. The man relished the brutal scene with violent excitement. For a whole minute the dogs mauled her mercilessly, before the man called them away. The hounds obeyed and withdrew from her reluctantly, snarling and salivating. With a careless flick, he asked the few people who had gathered around to take the woman away. Sniggering he turned away and called for a cup of hot coffee to be served to him.

She was taken to her outer quarters, where her folk surrounded her wiped her wounds and called for medical help. She had been bonded to the man for life since as far as she could remember. She was sold to him in her teens. To the woman, the man was her Master and the alien manor her home.

When I expressed my concern regarding the lizard, my mother pacified me telling that it did not have the sixth sense that it can anticipate fear.
